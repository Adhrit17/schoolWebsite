History

Castlio Elementary was named after Hiram Beverly Castlio, affectionately known as “Uncle Bev.” He was the nephew of Francis Howell, Jr., and one of three directors named in Howell’s will to build and supervise the Francis Howell Institute. Mr. Castlio served without pay in that position from the time the Institute opened its doors in 1881 until1895. Under his leadership and direction, it became the center of higher education in St. Charles County. At one time, it was estimated that more than half of the teachers in the county had attended Francis Howell Institute. When Mr. Castlio died in 1904, he bequeathed $10,000 to the Institute. His love for public education has continued on through our elementary school. Castlio Elementary School first opened on July 27, 1981. At the time of it’s opening, it included 20 regular classrooms, four special education classrooms, an art room, a resource center, an office, and a gym that also served as the cafeteria. Because the school was built on a 100-acre site, there were thoughts that a middle school or high school would be included in the future. However, that never occurred.

Whole Grade and Subject Matter Acceleration (Aug 29, 2017)
Acceleration is an intervention that moves students through an educational program at rates faster, or at younger ages, than typical. It means matching the level, complexity, and pace of the curriculum to the readiness and motivation of the student. All acceleration requires high academic ability. The student’s motivation, social-emotional maturity, and interests must be considered when making decisions about acceleration. The student whose level of achievement and ability significantly surpasses same age group peers as well as the student with high ability but who is not performing well in class are examples of who might be considered for subject-matter or whole-grade acceleration. The procedures described in the packet attached to this page are not intended to increase the number of requests for acceleration or to initiate a large number of adjustments to curriculum in most classrooms. Rather, they are for the exceptional situation in which on-going classroom accommodations for students with advanced ability are not sufficiently differentiated to make school a challenging place for the individual student to learn. It should be pointed out that all building principals are familiar with the Board of Education’s policies and regulations on acceleration and expect teachers to differentiate instruction in the classroom based on formal and informal assessments. Therefore, the expectation is that teachers are familiar with their students’ strengths and needs and successfully accommodate learning differences on an on-going basis in their classrooms. It is also expected that parents with concerns about academic challenge have worked with both the classroom teacher and building principal before taking the step of completing an acceleration request form.
FHHS's Becker Wins KMOX/SMSU Student of Achievement Award (Mar 15, 2018)
“Rarely does a student come along who will change the world,” said Darci Hillyer, school counselor for Francis Howell High School (FHHS). “Faith Becker is such a student.” Becker was recently awarded the KMOX/Southeast Missouri State University (SMSU) Student of Achievement Award. “Faith’s integrity, compassion, and leadership style have already changed our school and community,” Hillyer said. “Although Faith stands out academically, it is her purposeful life which distinguishes her from her peers.” And it certainly helped her win this prestigious award. Becker is a junior at FHHS, and when the school needs to select a representative from the student body, she usually gets the call. She is a member of several leadership groups which seek to promote positive change in our communities. Becker attends monthly diversity retreats “where students are encouraged to polish their leadership skills by discussing controversial topics like the Ferguson riots, impoverished areas of St. Louis, and discrimination and racism,” Hillyer said. “(Becker) is currently working with students in North County to bring our two high schools together on a fundraising effort in order to increase awareness about racial issues in our society.” These experiences have piqued Becker’s interest in studying political science in college, but she isn’t pursuing it for the prospects of future notoriety or monetary gain. Becker wants to get into politics to fight injustice in America. However, words are wind and action speaks far more loudly. When kids turn sixteen, many desire extravagant parties or expensive gifts that will mark their reaching of the milestone. It is their day, and they deserve to have the attention. Becker, on the other hand, directed the attention to something she felt was far more important than her Sweet 16 party – the plight of a neighboring community, which had been brought to the forefront by the tragic events in Ferguson in 2014. She decided to forego her Sweet 16, and instead raise scholarship funds for high school students in the Ferguson-Florissant School District. And with that, the Project 16 Fund was born, a non-profit subsidiary created with the Greater Saint Louis Community Foundation. Becker’s Project 16 Fund raised money through various activities and outlets, reaching its goal of six thousand dollars, and helping send three worthy students to college. “In all my years as a counselor,” Hillyer said, “I’ve never witnessed an event of this magnitude singlehandedly spearheaded by a high school student.” And after hearing of Becker’s award from KMOX and SMSU, Hillyer couldn’t be happier. She said, “I am immensely proud of Faith for her selfless service to our school and community. I am thrilled that Faith’s accomplishments are being recognized with this prestigious award. Faith is usually the one giving to others so it’s nice to find her on the receiving end for once!” Becker is honored, to say the least. “I feel very honored and privileged to receive such a generous scholarship,” she said, “and I would like to thank KMOX and SEMO for giving me this opportunity.”
SEGA Identification Process (Jan 26, 2016)
Students who meet the qualifications for the gifted program in the Francis Howell School District, and have a full scale IQ score in the 99th percentile or above will be referred for the SEGA program by the building Gifted Education Specialist. Referred students will be given a standardized achievement test in multiple academic areas. Most students who qualify for SEGA score 2 standard deviations above grade level in at least two of the three subtests given. Anecdotal information from the parents, classroom teacher and Gifted Education Specialist may be utilized to determine a child’s strengths, but is not a deciding factor in program placement. Students selected to participate in the program will be placed in SEGA at the beginning of the following school year for a one-year probationary period to assure that the program best fits the needs of the student.
